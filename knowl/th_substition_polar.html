Theorem 1.7.3. Polar change of variables.

The function

\begin{align*} G\colon \R^2 \amp\rightarrow \R^2 \\ (r,\theta)\amp \mapsto (\underset{x}{\underbrace{r\cos\theta}}, \underset{y}{\underbrace{r\sin\theta}}) \end{align*}
is continuously differentiable everywhere. The Jacobian of \(G\) is
\begin{equation} \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)}=\det\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta \amp -r\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta\amp r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}=r(\cos^2\theta+\sin\theta^2)=r\text{.}\tag{1.7.1} \end{equation}
Assume \(G\) is one-to-one on the interior of \(\mathcal{R}\subseteq\R^2\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{S}=G(\mathcal{R})\) be the image under \(G\text{.}\) If \(f\) is continuous on the interior of \(\mathcal{R}\text{,}\) then by TheoremÂ 1.6.6 we have
\begin{align} \iint\limits_\mathcal{S}f(x,y)\, dA \amp =\iint\limits_\mathcal{R}f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta) \, \abs{r}\, dA \tag{1.7.2}\\ \amp =\iint\limits_\mathcal{R}f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta) \, r\, dA \amp (\text{assuming } r\geq 0)\text{.}\tag{1.7.3} \end{align}
