Let \(\mathcal{S}\subseteq\R^3\) be a smoothly parametrized surface. Informally, an orientation of \(\mathcal{S}\) is a continuous choice of unit normal vectors to points on the surface. More precisely, an orientation is a continuous function

\begin{align*} \boldn \colon \mathcal{S}\amp\rightarrow \R^3 \\ (x,y,z)\amp\mapsto \boldn(x,y,z) \text{,} \end{align*}

where for each \(P=(x,y,z)\in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) \(\boldn(P)\) is a unit normal vector to \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(P\text{.}\) The surface \(\mathcal{S}\) is orientable if an orientation \(\boldn\colon \mathcal{S}\rightarrow \R^3\) exists for \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) If no orientation function exists, the surface is nonorientable.
