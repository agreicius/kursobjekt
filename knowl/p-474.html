Fix a constant \(R>1/2\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the Möbius strip with parametrization

\begin{equation*} \boldr(\theta, t)=\left((R+t\cos(\theta/2))\cos\theta, (R+t\cos(\theta/2))\sin\theta, t\sin(\theta/2\right)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\ 0\leq \theta\leq 2\pi, -\frac{1}{2}\leq t\leq\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\)

Fact: \(\mathcal{S}\) is a nonorientable surface! If this is so, why doesn't the function

\begin{equation*} P=\boldr(\theta_0, t_0)\mapsto \frac{\boldr_t\times \boldr_\theta(\theta_0,t_0)}{\abs{\boldr_\theta\times\boldr_t(\theta_0,t_0)}} \end{equation*}

provide us with an orientation? The answer: this is a well-defined function on the parameter domain \([0,2\pi]\times[-1/2,1/2]\text{,}\) but not on the actual surface \(\mathcal{S}\text{!}\) Indeed, the point \(P=(R,0,0)\) can be expressed as both \(\boldr(0,0)\) and \(\boldr(2\pi, 0)\text{.}\) As the Sage cell below illustrates the two normal vectors corresponding to \((0,0)\) and \((2\pi,0)\) are \(\angvec{1,0,0}\) and \(\angvec{-1,0,0}\text{!}\)
