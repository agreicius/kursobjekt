Solution 1.9.3.1.

It is inuitively clear from the symmetry of \(D\text{,}\) as well as the fact that \(\delta(x,y,z)=z\) does not depend on \(x\) or \(y\text{,}\) that \(M_{yz}=M_{xz}=0\text{.}\) In more detail let \(D=D_1\cup D_2\) where \(D_1\) is the right half of \(D\) (i.e., where \(x\geq 0\)), and \(D_2\) is the left half. Let \(f(x,y,z)=x\delta(x,y,z)=xz\text{.}\) The transformation \(G(x,y,z)=(-x,y,z)\) maps \(D_1\) onto \(D_2\text{.}\) By substitution we have

\begin{align*} \iiint_{D_2}f(x,y,z)\, dV\amp =\iiint_{D_1}f(-x,y,z)\abs{J(G)}\, dV\\ \amp =\iiint_{D_1}-xz\, dV\\ \amp =-\iiint_{D_1}f(x,y,z)\, dV\text{,} \end{align*}

and thus

\begin{align*} M_{yz} \amp=\iiint_D xz\, dV \\ \amp=\iiint_{D_1}xz\, dV+\iiint_{D_2}xz\, dV \\ \amp =\iiint_{D_1}xz\, dV-\iiint_{D_1}xz\, dV=0\text{.} \end{align*}

A similar argument works to show \(M_x=0\text{.}\)

To compute the remaining ingredients we will use spherical coordinates. Observe that the solid hemisphere \(D\) has spherical description

\begin{equation*} \{(\rho, \phi,\theta)\colon 0\leq \theta\leq 2\pi, 0\leq \phi\leq \pi/2, 0\leq \rho\leq 2\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now compute:

\begin{align*} M \amp=\iiint_D \delta(x,y,z)\, dV \\ \amp =\iiint_{D}z\, dV\\ \amp =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2\rho\cos\phi\cdot \rho^2\sin\phi \amp \text{(spher. subst.)}\\ \amp = 2\pi \int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\phi\sin\phi\, d\phi\int_0^2\rho^3\, d\rho\\ \amp =\frac{2}{8}\pi(\sin^2\phi)\vert_0^{\pi/2}(\rho^4)\vert_0^{2}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{4}\pi 2^4=4\pi\\ M_{xy} \amp =\iiint_D z\delta(x,y,z)\, dV \\ \amp =\iiint_{D}z^2\, dV\\ \amp =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2\rho^2\cos^2\phi\cdot \rho^2\sin\phi \amp \text{(spher. subst.)}\\ \amp = 2\pi \int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^2\phi\sin\phi\, d\phi\int_0^2\rho^4\, d\rho\\ \amp =\frac{2}{15}\pi(-\cos^3\phi)\vert_0^{\pi/2}(\rho^5)\vert_0^{2}\\ \amp =\frac{2}{15}\pi 2^5=\frac{64}{15}\pi\\ (\overline{x},\overline{y},\overline{z}) \amp = (0,0,M_{xy}/M)\\ \amp = \left(0,0,\frac{16}{15}\right)\text{.} \end{align*}
