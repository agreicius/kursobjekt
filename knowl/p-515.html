Note that in this case we computed the surface integral by (a) identifying the outward orientation \(\boldn\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) geometrically (as opposed to using \(\boldr_\phi\times \boldr_\theta\) or its opposite), (b) computing the scalar function \(\boldF(x,y,z)\cdot \boldn(x,y,z)\text{,}\) and (c) integrating with respect to \(d\sigma=\vert \boldr_\phi\times\boldr_\theta\vert=4\sin\phi\text{.}\) This method works in this case because we could easily identify the orientation function \(\boldn(x,y,z)\text{.}\) When this is not easily done, resort to the procedure indicated in Theorem 2.6.15.
