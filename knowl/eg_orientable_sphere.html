Example 2.6.10. Orientations of the sphere.

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the unit sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\text{.}\) We have seen previously that for a point \(P=(x,y,z)\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) the vector \(\overrightarrow{OP}=\angvec{x,y,z}\) is a normal vector to \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(P\text{;}\) furthermore, since \(P\in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) its magnitude is \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}=1\text{.}\) Thus the function \(\boldn_1(x,y,z)=\angvec{x,y,z}\) assigns a unit normal vector to each point \(P=(x,y,z)\in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Since \(\boldn_1\) is clearly continuous, it defines an orientation on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) called the outward orientation. The function \(\boldn_2(x,y,z)=-\angvec{x,y,z}\) also defines an orientation on the sphere, called the inward orientation.

More generally, if \(\mathcal{S}\) is a smooth surface that is the boundary of a bounded solid region \(D\subseteq\R^3\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{S}\) has both an outward and inward orientation. In particular, all such surfaces are orientable. The sphere discussed above is one such surface: it is the boundary of the solid ball \(x^2+y^2+z^2\leq 1\text{.}\) Another example is the torus: the boundary surface of a solid doughnut.
