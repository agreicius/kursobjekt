Remark 1.6.7. Substitution as change of variable.

Substitution is often described as a change of variable technique. Let's see why, treating the \(n=2\) case. Treat the region \(D\) as living in the \(uv\)-plane, and the region \(D\) as living in the \(xy\)-plane. Given a transformation \(G=(g_1,g_2)\) from \(D\) to \(D'\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align} x \amp=g_1(u,v) \tag{1.6.5}\\ y \amp=g_2(u,v) \text{.}\tag{1.6.6} \end{align}

Furthermore, since \(G\) is one-to-one and onto we can solve for \(u\) and \(v\) in terms of \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) obtaining expressions

\begin{align} u \amp=h_1(x,y) \tag{1.6.7}\\ v \amp=h_2(x,y) \text{.}\tag{1.6.8} \end{align}

With this notation, (1.6.1) can be expressed as

\begin{equation*} \iint\limits_\mathcal{D'}f(x,y)\, dA=\iint\limits_\mathcal{D'}f(g_1(u,v),g_2(u,v))\abs{\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}}\, dA\text{.} \end{equation*}

We think of this equation as asserting that the original (or given) integral \(\iint\limits_{D'}f(x,y)\, dA\) can be computed via the change of variables
