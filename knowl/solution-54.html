Solution 2.6.16.1.

  • Parametrize. Using cylindrical coordinates, we see that \(\mathcal{S}\) has parametrization

    \begin{align*} \bolds(r,\theta) \amp = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r)\\ \mathcal{R}\colon 0\amp\leq \theta\leq 2\pi, 0\leq r\leq 4 \text{.} \end{align*}

  • Ingredients.

    \begin{align*} \bolds_r \amp = \angvec{\cos\theta, \sin\theta, 1}\\ \bolds_\theta \amp= \angvec{-r\sin\theta, r\cos\theta, 0} \\ \bolds_r\times\bolds_\theta \amp =\angvec{-r\cos\theta, -r\sin\theta, r} \text{.} \end{align*}

    Observe that since \(r\geq 0\text{,}\) the normal vector \(\bolds_r\times\bolds_\theta\) points upward, and hence toward the \(z\)-axis. Thus the outward orientation \(\boldn\) comes from \(\bolds_\theta\times\bolds_r\text{.}\)

  • Now compute

    \begin{align*} \iint_\mathcal{S}\boldF\cdot\boldn\, d\sigma \amp = \iint_\mathcal{R}\boldF(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r)\cdot \angvec{r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta, -r}\, dA \\ \amp =\iint_\mathcal{R}\angvec{r^2\cos\theta\sin\theta,0,-r}\cdot\angvec{r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta, -r}\, dA \\ \amp =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4r^3\cos^2\theta\sin\theta+r^2\, dr\, d\theta\\ \amp = \frac{128}{3}\pi\text{.} \end{align*}
