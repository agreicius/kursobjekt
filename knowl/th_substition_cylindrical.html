Theorem 1.7.9. Cylindrical change of variables.

The function

\begin{align*} G\colon \R^3 \amp\rightarrow \R^3 \\ (r,\theta, z)\amp \mapsto (\underset{x}{\underbrace{r\cos\theta}}, \underset{y}{\underbrace{r\sin\theta}}, z) \end{align*}
is continuously differentiable everywhere. The Jacobian of \(G\) is
\begin{equation} \frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(r,\theta,z)}=\det\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta \amp -r\sin\theta\amp 0\\ \sin\theta\amp r\cos\theta\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 1 \end{pmatrix}=r(\cos^2\theta+\sin\theta^2)=r\text{.}\tag{1.7.5} \end{equation}
Assume \(G\) is one-to-one on the interior of \(\mathcal{R}\subseteq\R^3\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{S}=G(\mathcal{R})\) be the image under \(G\text{.}\) If \(f\) is continuous on the interior of \(\mathcal{R}\text{,}\) then by TheoremÂ 1.6.6 we have
\begin{align} \iiint\limits_\mathcal{S}f(x,y,z)\, dV \amp =\iiint\limits_\mathcal{R}f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z) \, \abs{r}\, dV \tag{1.7.6}\\ \amp =\iiint\limits_\mathcal{R}f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z) \, r\, dV \amp (\text{assuming } r\geq 0) \text{.}\tag{1.7.7} \end{align}
