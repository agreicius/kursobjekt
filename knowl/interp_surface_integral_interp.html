Interpretation 2.6.5. Surface integral interpretation.

As always, the key to understanding the meaning of a surface integral is to make sense of the right-hand side of the approximation formula

\begin{equation} \iint\limits_\mathcal{S}f\, d\sigma\approx \sum_{k=1}^{n}f(\boldr(u_k,v_k))\underset{\operatorname{approx. area of} \Delta\sigma_k}{\underbrace{\abs{\boldr_u\times \boldr_v}\Delta u\Delta v}}\text{.}\tag{2.6.1} \end{equation}

Understanding \(\abs{\boldr_u\times \boldr_v}\Delta u\Delta v\) as an approximation of the area of one of the subpatches of \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) the interpretation then depends on what the function \(f\) is. Here are two typical examples.

  1. Geometric: surface area.

    If \(f=1\) (the constant function), then the right-hand side of (2.6.1) simply adds up \(\abs{\boldr_u\times \boldr_v}\Delta u\Delta v\text{,}\) yielding an approximation of the area of \(S\text{.}\) The integral of \(f=1\) computes this area exactly.

  2. Physical: density function.

    Assume \(f(x,y,z)\geq 0\) gives us the density of quantity \(Q\) per unit area at position \((x,y,z)\text{.}\) In this case the right-hand side of (2.6.1) can be understood as an approximation of the total quantity \(Q\) over \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) and thus the integral computes this total quantity exactly.
