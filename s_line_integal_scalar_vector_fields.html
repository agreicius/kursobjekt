Section 2.2 Line integrals of vector fields

Subsection 2.2.1 Vector fields

Definition 2.2.1. Vector fields.

A vector field is a function

\begin{align*} \boldF\colon \R^n \amp \rightarrow \R^n\\ (x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\amp\mapsto \left\langle F_1\langle x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n),F_2(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n),\dots ,F_n(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\right\rangle \text{.} \end{align*}

The scalar functions \(F_i\) are called the component functions. The vector field \(\boldF\) is continuous, differentiable, continuously differentiable, etc., if each of the component functions is continuous, differentiable, continuously differentiable, etc.

Procedure 2.2.2. Visualizing vector fields.

Like a curve parametrization, a vector field \(\boldF\colon \R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) is yet another example of a vector-valued function. In contrast to parametrizations, however, note that both the input and output of \(\boldF\) are \(n\)-tuples. This makes visualizing \(F\) slightly more complicated. We use the following method to do so. As always, \(n\) is equal to 2 or 3 for us.

  1. Interpret the input tuple \(P=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) as a point in \(n\)-space.

  2. Treat the output tuple \(\boldv=\boldF(P)=\langle F_1(P),F_2(P),\dots, F_n(P)\rangle \) as an \(n\)-vector.

  3. For a given input \(P\in \R^n\text{,}\) draw the output \(\boldv=\boldF(P)\) as a vector with initial point equal to \(P\text{:}\) i.e., the “tail” of the vector \(\boldF(P)\) is chosen to be the point \(P\text{.}\)

  4. Sketch the entire vector field by performing Step 3 for many input points!

Subsection 2.2.2 Line integrals of vector fields

Definition 2.2.3. Integral of a vector field along a curve.

Let \(\boldr\colon \R\rightarrow \R^n\) be a smooth parametrization of the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\boldT(t)=\boldr'(t)/\abs{\boldr'(t)}\) be the unit tangent vector of \(\boldr\text{.}\) Given a vector field \(\boldF\colon \R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) that is continuous on the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq \R^n\text{,}\) we define its line integral, denoted \(\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\text{,}\) as

\begin{align} \int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\amp=\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot\boldT\, ds\tag{2.2.1}\\ \amp =\int_a^b\boldF(\boldr(t))\cdot\frac{\boldr'(t)}{\abs{\boldr'(t)}}\, \abs{\boldr'(t)}\, dt \tag{2.2.2}\\ \amp = \int_a^b\boldF(\boldr(t))\cdot \boldr'(t)\, dt\text{.}\tag{2.2.3} \end{align}

Remark 2.2.4. Vector field line integral notation.

The first equality among (2.2.1)–(2.2.3) should be considered purely notational: i.e., it states simply that the integral being defined can be denoted by either of the expressions \(\int_C \boldF\cdot d\boldr\) or \(\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot \boldT\, ds\text{.}\) The remaining equalities (2.2.2)–(2.2.3) then give actual meaning to these two expressions.

To make sense of what a vector field line integral computes in a given context, it is best to consider its formulation as the scalar line integral of the scalar function \(\boldF\cdot \boldT\text{.}\) Given a partition of a parametrized curve into curve segments with endpoints

\begin{equation*} P=\boldr(a), P_1=\boldr(t_1),\dots, P_{n-1}=\boldr(t_{n-1}), P_n=\boldr(t_n) \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot T\, ds\approx \sum_{k=1}^n \boldF(x_k, y_k, z_k)\cdot \boldT(x_k, y_k, z_k)\Delta s_k, \end{equation*}

where \((x_k,y_k,z_k)\) is a sample point in the \(k\)-th segment of the partition of \(\mathcal{C}\text{,}\) and \(\Delta s_k\) is the arc length of that segment. Focusing in on one term \(\boldF(x_k, y_k, z_k)\cdot \boldT(x_k, y_k, z_k)\Delta s_k\) and recalling our dot product cosine formula

\begin{equation*} \boldF\cdot \boldT=\abs{\boldF}\abs{\boldT}\cos\theta\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\boldF\) and \(\boldT\) at \((x_k, y_k, z_k)\text{,}\) we see that this term multiplies the component of \(\boldF\) in the direction of \(\boldT\) by the arc length \(\Delta s_k\) of the \(k\)-th segment. We have thus reduced our interpretation problem to understanding what type of quantity is obtained by summing these products. The definition of work and circulation below are two examples of doing just this.

Remark 2.2.7. Independence of parametrization.

Let \(\boldr\) and \(\bolds\) be two smooth parametrizations of the curve \(\mathcal{C}\) between the points \(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\)

  1. Denote by \(\mathcal{C}_1\) the curve \(\mathcal{C}\) with parametrization \(\boldr\text{,}\) and \(\mathcal{C}_2\) the curve \(\mathcal{C}\) with parametrization \(\bolds\text{.}\) If \(\boldr, \bolds\) both traverse \(\mathcal{C}\) exactly once, and in the same direction (e.g., both start at \(P\) and end at \(Q\)), then

    \begin{equation*} \int_{\mathcal{C}_1}\boldF\cdot d\boldr=\int_{\mathcal{C}_2}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\text{.} \end{equation*}

  2. If \(\boldr, \bolds\) both traverse \(\mathcal{C}\) exactly once, but \(\bolds\) starts where \(\boldr\) ends, and vice-versa, then we call the parametrization \(\bolds\) a reverse of \(\boldr\text{.}\) Denote by \(\mathcal{C}\) the curve as parametrized by \(\boldr\text{,}\) \(-\mathcal{C}\) the curve as parametrized by the reverse path \(\bolds\text{.}\) We have

    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\mathcal{C}}\boldF\cdot d\boldr=-\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\text{.} \end{equation*}

Definition 2.2.9. Line integrals with respect to \(dx, dy, dz\).

Let \(\boldr(t)=(g_1(t),g_2(t),g_3(t))\text{,}\) \(a\leq t\leq b\text{,}\) be a smooth parametrization of the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq\R^n\text{.}\) Given a vector field \(\boldF=(F_1, F_2,F_3)\) that is continuous on the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq \R^n\text{,}\) we define the line integrals with respect to \(dx, dy, dz\) as follows.

\begin{align*} \int_\mathcal{C}F_1\, dx \amp :=\int_a^b F_1(\boldr(t))\, g_1'(t)\, dt \\ \int_\mathcal{C}F_2\, dy \amp :=\int_a^b F_2(\boldr(t))\, g_2'(t)\, dt \\ \int_\mathcal{C}F_3\, dz \amp :=\int_a^b F_3(\boldr(t))\, g_3'(t)\, dt \text{.} \end{align*}

From Definition 2.2.3 we see that we have

\begin{align*} \int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr \amp =\int_\mathcal{C}F_1\, dx +\int_\mathcal{C}F_2\, dy +\int_\mathcal{C}F_3\, dz \\ \amp=\int_\mathcal{C}F_1\, dx+F_2\, dy+F_3\, dz \amp \text{(abbreviated notation)} \text{.} \end{align*}

Definition 2.2.10. Work and flow.

Let \(\boldr\colon [a,b]\rightarrow \R^m\) be a a smooth parametrization of the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq\R^n\text{,}\) and assume the vector field \(\boldF\colon \R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) is continuous on \(\mathcal{C}\text{.}\)

  1. If \(\boldF(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) is the force acting at the point \((x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\text{,}\) then \(\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\) is the work done in moving an object along the path \(\boldr\) from \(\boldr(a)\) to \(\boldr(b)\text{.}\)

  2. If \(\boldF(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) is the velocity vector of a fluid at the point \((x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\text{,}\) then \(\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\) is the flow along the path \(\boldr\) from \(\boldr(a)\) to \(\boldr(b)\text{.}\) The path \(\boldr\) is simple (one-to-one except possibly at the endpoints) and closed (\(\boldr(a)=\boldr(b)\)), then \(\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot d\boldr\) is called the circulation around \(\mathcal{C}\text{.}\)

Definition 2.2.12. Flux.

Let \(\boldr=(g_1(t),g_2(t))\text{,}\) \(a\leq t\leq b\) be a smooth simple closed parametrization of the curve \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq\R^2\text{,}\) and assume \(\boldF=(F_1,F_2)\) is continuous on \(\mathcal{C}\text{.}\) For all \(a\leq t\leq b\text{,}\) the unit normal vector to \(\mathcal{C}\) with positive orientation is defined as

\begin{equation*} \boldn(t)=\frac{\angvec{g_2'(t),-g_1'(t)}}{\sqrt{g_1^2(t)+g_2^2(t)}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The flux of \(\boldF\) across \(\mathcal{C}\) is defined as

\begin{align*} \operatorname{Flux}_\mathcal{C} \boldF \amp =\int_\mathcal{C}\boldF\cdot \boldn \, ds \\ \amp=\int_a^b\boldF(\boldr(t))\cdot \boldn(t) \abs{\boldr'(t)}\, dt \\ \amp = \int_a^b F_1(\boldr(t))g_2'(t)-F_2(\boldr(t))g_1'(t)\, dt\\ \amp = \oint_\mathcal{C} F_1\, dy-F_2\, dx\text{.} \end{align*}
